Resource Utilization for Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 11

Problem Statement

SDG 11 focuses on making cities and human settlements safe, effective, and sustainable. This business case study highlights the utilization of natural resources such as solar, wind, water, and waste, aligning with SDG 11. These resources are often underutilized, leading to pollution, energy shortages, and inefficient waste management. We aim to decode how urban areas can utilize these resources efficiently to create safe, healthy, and sustainable cities.

Solutions in Prakasavan

Prakasavan, with its industrial areas, metro, railway, high-rise apartments, and luxury centers, can thrive sustainably by utilizing natural resources effectively.

1. Solar Energy

By equipping rooftops, public infrastructure, and urban spaces with solar panels, this city can generate clean energy while reducing dependence on fossil fuels.

Example: Bangalore, India, implemented a solar incentive program, encouraging residential solar installations and saving 150 MW of electricity annually (Source: Ministry of New and Renewable Energy, India).

2. Wind Energy

Urban areas can utilize small-scale wind turbines or hybrid solar-wind systems to meet localized energy demands.

Example: London‚Äôs Olympic Park integrates wind turbines into its sustainability plan, supplying renewable energy to public infrastructure (Source: UK Department for Business, Energy & Industrial Strategy).

3. Water Management

Rainwater harvesting, greywater recycling, and small-scale hydropower systems can manage water resources efficiently.

Example: Singapore‚Äôs NEWater program recycles treated wastewater into potable water, meeting 40% of the nation‚Äôs water demand (Source: Singapore‚Äôs Public Utilities Board).

4. Waste-to-Resource

Organic waste can be converted into biogas or compost, while non-organic waste is recycled or used for energy generation.

Example: Sweden transforms over 99% of its household waste into energy and other usable forms, drastically reducing its reliance on landfills (Source: Swedish Waste Management Association).

Data and Analytics

  • Solar Energy: California generated 32% of its electricity from solar energy in 2022, reducing CO‚ÇÇ emissions by 28 million metric tons annually (Source: California Energy Commission).
  • Wind Energy: Denmark relies on wind energy for 50% of its electricity, significantly reducing its carbon footprint (Source: Danish Energy Agency).
  • Water Management: Australia's urban water-sensitive programs save 50 billion liters of water annually (Source: Australian Bureau of Statistics).
  • Waste-to-Resource: South Korea recycles 95% of its food waste, producing bioenergy and organic fertilizer (Source: Korea Environment Institute).

What We Can Learn and Implement in Prakasavan

  • Launch a Solar Roof Initiative with low-interest loans for solar panel installation. Equip public buildings with solar panels and create solar-powered streetlights.
  • Identify windy locations for small wind turbine installation and combine solar and wind systems for uninterrupted energy.
  • Implement rainwater harvesting systems, set up greywater recycling plants, and plan reservoirs to store and reuse water.
  • Introduce waste segregation with different bins for organic and inorganic waste, and build biogas plants to convert organic waste into fuel.
  • Foster community involvement through educational events, gamification, rewards, and partnerships with local businesses to build a sustainable city culture.

Benefits of Resource Utilization for SDG 11

  • Reduces greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Enhances urban growth while mitigating climate change impacts.
  • Creates economic opportunities through green jobs.

Affordable Housing in Prakashavan

Problem Statement

Prakashavan is a growing municipality with 5 lakhs as the population and ‚Çπ5 lakhs as the yearly average household income. Serious housing affordability issues have engulfed the city:

  • Growing Population at 15 percent in the last ten years has surpassed the growth in its housing supply.
  • Median Rent of over ‚Çπ20,000 a month (source: Hindustan Times), the cost accounting for nearly an entire third of the earned incomes.
  • Rise of Informal Settlements: 12% of the population lives in substandard housing. (source: Prakashavan Statistics)
  • Gentrification: The rise of high-income groups has led to rising property prices, displacing low-income families. (source: Wikipedia)

Real-world Examples: Vienna, Austria faced housing shortages and rising rents during rapid urbanization in the mid-20th century. Bhopal, India experienced housing deficits due to urban migration and unplanned growth. Cape Town, South Africa struggled with informal settlements and a growing population post-apartheid (source: Deccan Herald).

Solution

Inspired by successful models from cities like Vienna, Bhopal, and Cape Town, Prakashavan brought in the following strategies:

  • Affordable Housing Policies:
    • Implementation of rent control policies to keep annual rent increases up to 5% (controls inflation too).
    • Legislations requiring developers to provide 30% of new units under affordable housing.
  • Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs): Partner with private builders to build 10,000 subsidized units within five years. Developers get tax benefits and instant clearances for affordable projects (example: Bhopal).
  • Urban Renewal Projects: Transform wasted government land into affordable dwellings. Resettle families from slums into organized mixed-use communities.
  • Benefits for Citizens:
    • Benefits to first-time homemakers with incomes less than ‚Çπ3 lakhs.
    • Introduction of housing loans at lower interest rates for middle-class families.
    • Under the Karnataka government‚Äôs ASHRAYA scheme, providing ‚Çπ1.5 lakh to ‚Çπ2.5 lakh per house to BPL holders.
  • Innovative Housing Designs:
    • Low-cost modular housing inspired by prefab systems of Singapore, reducing construction costs by 20-30%.
    • Green and energy-efficient buildings to cut down utility costs, aiding sustainability.

Data and Analysis

Pre-Implementation

  • Housing deficit: ~20,000 units.
  • Average monthly rent: ‚Çπ22,000 (~44% of average income).
  • Informal settlement population: ~60,000 people.

After 5 Years of Initiatives

  • 12,000 affordable units constructed, reducing the housing deficit by 60%.
  • Informal settlement population decreased by 30% due to relocation projects.
  • Rents in affordable units reduced to ‚Çπ15,000, making housing affordable for middle-class families.
  • Rent without intervention projected to be ‚Çπ30,000/month (~60% of household income).

Ideas Inspired By: Vienna‚Äôs public housing, Bhopal‚Äôs smart city initiative, and Cape Town‚Äôs integration model reduced social inequality and enhanced housing accessibility.

Conclusion

  • Inclusive Planning: Community involvement decreased resistance to relocation.
  • Affordability: Rent caps ensured long-term housing affordability.
  • Economic Boost: Increased disposable income led to a 15% increase in local spending (calculated using ExpenseCount.com).

Challenges:

  • Funding Shortfalls: Initial state government funding was inadequate.
  • Land Acquisition: Legal disputes delayed redevelopment projects.
  • Hesitation from Developers: Private sector involvement was hindered by fears of profit loss.

Urban Challenges and Solutions

Problem Statement

  • High Pollution: Cities emit significant amounts of air and noise pollutants from automobiles, factories, and energy consumption, which pollutes the environment and endangers human health.
  • Overuse of Resources: Cities consume too much water, energy, and land resources, which causes damage to nature and leads to shortages.
  • Traffic and Congestion: Cities cause heavy traffic, which leads to pollution, waste of time, and dirty air, all of which negatively affect the health of people and the environment.
  • Waste Problems: Cities generate so much waste that most cities don't have a proper recycling or disposal system, resulting in overflowing garbage and pollution.
  • Loss of Nature: The growth of cities takes away the parks, forests, and other green areas of the nature. This will reduce the wildlife and the environment gets hotter.
  • Water Issues: Cities also pollute the water bodies through wastes from the industries and the households, and utilize excess water causing shortages and bad quality.
  • Ineffective Buildings: Most buildings in the cities waste energy since they are not constructed to be effective in energy use, increasing pollution and harming the environment.
  • Vulnerability to Climate Change: Cities may not always be prepared for climate change, such as a heatwave or flood that can damage infrastructure and cause harm to residents.

    • Conserve Energy: Fit energy-saving bulbs and install solar panels to reduce energy wastage.

    • Conserve Water: Install low-flow faucets and encourage people to collect rainwater.

    • Make Streets Safer for Walking and Cycling: Increase the number of pathways for walking and cycling to reduce car traffic.
  • Waste Problems:
    • Recycle More: Establish more recycling bins and teach people how to sort trash.
    • Compost Food Waste: Teach people to compost food scraps rather than throwing them away.
  • Loss of Nature:
    • More Parks and Gardens: Construct more green areas where people can relax and appreciate nature.

Environmental Impacts in Urban Areas

Problem Statement

There are several reasons why negative environmental impacts have been realized in urban areas, including limited public spaces, lack of sustainable transportation, air and noise pollution, traffic congestion, flooding caused by improper drainage systems, and climate change. All these factors make urbanization worsen the conditions by overpopulation and decrease in quality life.

Solution

Limited Public Spaces:

  • Develop more accessible and affordable open spaces that can be used by the public, such as parks and gardens.

Sustainable Transportation:

  • Expand public transport systems like buses, metros, and cycling lanes.
  • Promote walking and reduce the use of personal vehicles by promoting electric vehicles (EVs).

Air and Noise Pollution:

  • Shift to electric vehicles to reduce emissions.
  • Reduce industrial and vehicular noise pollution by enforcing strict regulations.

Traffic Management:

  • Promote public transport over private cars.
  • Designate areas for walking and cycling to enhance urban mobility.

Flooding Issues:

  • Implement proper drainage systems to manage rainwater effectively.
  • Maintain green spaces to absorb rainwater and prevent urban flooding.

Climate Change:

  • Use sustainable urban planning that has low carbon footprints.
  • Make crowded places car-free to reduce emissions.

Government Initiatives:

  • Set up budgeting for building infrastructure supporting public transport and non-motorized mobility.
  • Start campaigns that make people change their behavior.

Data and Analytics

  • Air Pollution Statistics: 52% of small particle pollution comes from vehicular emissions; if these were changed to EVs, then it would reduce by 15%.
  • Traffic Data: Only 8% of people in India own cars, and yet traffic congestion is rampant because of inadequate public transport and infrastructure.
  • Flood Insight: Poor drainage system and too much concrete areas lead to urban flooding in India during heavy rains.

Lesson Learnt

  • Reducing environmental degradation requires sustainable urban planning.
  • Public participation and behavior changes are necessary for the success of green initiatives.
  • Strategic interventions by the government can help transition to cities that are sustainable.
  • Data-driven insights reveal where there is a scope to improve and work on.

Conclusion

Targeted solutions like expansions in public spaces, infrastructures, and transport in green can reduce the urban footprints to a remarkable level. It requires efforts from all parties: government and business along with the masses.

Safiya H Discuss about the different studied algorithm design techniques.
  • Kruskal's Algorithm: Greedy approach which chooses the smallest edges in non-decreasing order, so no cycles are formed. Best for sparse graphs (few edges).
  • Prim's Algorithm: Greedy approach that grows the spanning tree by choosing the smallest edge connecting a vertex in the tree to one not in the tree. Best for dense graphs (many edges).
  • Dijkstra's Algorithm: Greedy approach that computes the shortest path from a source to all other nodes. Works best for graphs with non-negative weights.
  • Bellman-Ford Algorithm: Iterative approach that computes shortest paths, even with negative edge weights. The value really comes when detecting negative weight cycles.
  • Floyd-Warshall Algorithm: DP solution to find shortest paths between all pairs of nodes. Effective when dealing with small to medium-sized dense graphs.
